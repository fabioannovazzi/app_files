# Advisory Output Shaper

Use this skill when the task is not merely to summarize material, but to make an
advisor more effective in a real decision conversation.

The output should help someone conduct the next meeting, clarify choices, reduce
unproductive tension, and move toward a decision without hiding evidence limits.

## Core Rule

Do not confuse completeness with usefulness.

A useful advisory output separates:

- the **workpaper layer**: raw evidence, quotes, contradictions, doubts, source
  mapping, detailed analysis, and internal judgement;
- the **conversation layer**: neutral synthesis, shared ground, named tensions,
  options, decision criteria, facilitation questions, and next steps;
- the **backup layer**: technical detail, examples, precedents, benchmarks,
  legal/governance references, calculations, and source detail available if the
  conversation needs it.

Never push all three layers into the visible client output by default.

## Evidence Discipline

Before shaping the output, identify:

- what is directly observed in sources;
- what is inferred from those observations;
- what remains unknown or unresolved;
- which claims are safe to use in a client-facing or meeting-facing setting.

If source evidence has not been inspected, say so and inspect it before making
claims about the case, people, organization, or recommended path.

## Workflow

### 1. Define The Use Moment

Determine, from the request and source material:

- audience: advisor, client, family, board, management team, owners, lenders, or
  other stakeholders;
- setting: live meeting, pre-read, decision memo, follow-up note, workpaper,
  board pack, workshop, or presentation;
- decision stage: align, diagnose, choose, approve, negotiate, implement, or
  monitor;
- sensitivity: interpersonal conflict, role ambiguity, ownership tension,
  leadership transition, governance design, performance issue, or confidential
  evidence.

If these are not explicit, infer conservatively from the material and state the
assumption only when it affects the output.

### 2. Build The Workpaper Spine

Create or mentally maintain an internal spine:

- key facts;
- source-backed tensions;
- stakeholder positions or needs;
- decision constraints;
- evidence gaps;
- risks if the issue is not addressed;
- possible paths forward.

This spine can be direct and analytical. It is not automatically the visible
output.

### 3. Convert Raw Evidence Into Room-Safe Synthesis

For client-facing or group-facing output:

- start with shared ground before tensions;
- translate personal criticism into structural or procedural issues;
- use quotes sparingly and only when they help the room;
- avoid making one person the villain of a system problem;
- distinguish intention, behavior, role, and governance design;
- name disagreement as a decision problem, not a character problem.

Prefer formulations such as:

- "The current decision process leaves room for recurring ambiguity..."
- "The roles are not yet separated enough to make accountability visible..."
- "The evidence points to a need for explicit rules before the next phase..."

Avoid formulations such as:

- "The family is dysfunctional..."
- "The founder refuses to let go..."
- "The next generation is not ready..."
- "The plan is weak..."

Use sharper language only in the workpaper layer or when the user explicitly
asks for a private critique.

### 4. Use Role Lenses

Many advisory problems become clearer when roles are separated. Select the
lenses that fit the case:

- family or relationship role;
- owner/shareholder role;
- board or governance role;
- manager/executive role;
- technical/operator role;
- external stakeholder role.

Do not force all lenses into every output. Use them when they clarify why the
same person can have conflicting incentives or expectations.

### 5. Turn Values Into Operating Consequences

When a source mentions values, identity, continuity, trust, fairness, legacy, or
professionalization, do not leave them abstract.

Translate them into:

- observable behaviors;
- decision rules;
- reserved matters;
- role boundaries;
- meeting cadence;
- escalation paths;
- metrics or review moments;
- criteria for hiring, delegation, ownership, dividends, or investment.

Useful advisory output shows how values change decisions.

### 6. Frame Options By Conditions

Do not present scenarios as preferences too early. For each scenario, define:

- what it is;
- when it works;
- why it fails;
- minimum conditions;
- evidence needed before choosing it;
- decisions that cannot be postponed;
- first implementation test.

Use this structure when comparing leadership models, governance designs,
ownership options, board structures, operating models, succession paths, or
conflict-resolution mechanisms.

### 7. Make Conflict Procedural

Do not stop at "manage the conflict" or "improve communication."

When conflict, deadlock, recurring dissent, or role overlap appears, propose
procedural mechanisms such as:

- decision-rights map;
- reserved matters;
- majority, qualified-majority, unanimity, or consent rules;
- written dissent and reasoned objection;
- second meeting after a reflection period;
- independent technical expert;
- mediator or facilitator;
- board chair or independent director role;
- escalation ladder;
- family council or owner forum;
- buy-sell, exit, or liquidity mechanism;
- arbitration or formal dispute process as a last resort.

Separate technical disagreement from relational disagreement. They need
different procedures.

### 8. Add A Facilitation Layer

For any meeting-facing output, include practical prompts:

- questions to open the discussion;
- questions that test real agreement;
- choices the room can decide now;
- choices that need more evidence;
- topics that should not be forced prematurely;
- where the advisor should slow down, summarize, or park a disagreement.

An advisory deck or memo is more useful when it helps run the conversation, not
only when it displays analysis.

### 9. Design Main Output And Backup

Keep the visible output focused:

- 3-5 shared observations;
- the decision problem;
- the option set;
- conditions and tests;
- discussion questions;
- next steps.

Move detail to backup:

- raw evidence;
- long quotes;
- detailed source mapping;
- technical rules;
- benchmark examples;
- precedent cases;
- legal or financial detail;
- alternative formulations.

For slide decks, hidden or appendix slides are often useful when the advisor may
need support during discussion but should not force that detail into the main
story.

## Output Patterns

### Advisory Memo

Recommended structure:

1. Situation and decision need.
2. Shared facts.
3. Tensions to resolve.
4. Options and conditions.
5. Recommended next conversation.
6. Evidence gaps and risks.
7. Appendix or workpaper references.

### Meeting Deck

Recommended structure:

1. Purpose of meeting.
2. What we heard or observed.
3. Shared ground.
4. Tensions or open choices.
5. Conceptual frame needed for the discussion.
6. Options/scenarios with conditions.
7. Discussion prompts.
8. Next steps.
9. Backup.

### Workpaper

Recommended structure:

1. Source inventory.
2. Evidence map.
3. Direct observations.
4. Inferences.
5. Contradictions.
6. Advisor judgement candidates.
7. Client-safe synthesis candidates.
8. Open questions.

## Quality Gate

Before delivering, check:

- Is the output useful for the next real conversation?
- Does it separate evidence from inference?
- Does it avoid exposing raw tension unnecessarily?
- Does it make decisions and trade-offs explicit?
- Does every scenario include conditions, failure modes, and evidence needs?
- Does conflict have a procedure, not just a principle?
- Are values translated into operating consequences?
- Is the main output shorter and clearer than the workpaper?
- Is backup available where detail may be needed?
- Are unknowns visible enough to prevent false confidence?

If the answer to any of these is no, revise before delivery.

## Common Failure Modes

- Producing a polished summary that hides the real decision.
- Listing issues without designing the conversation that must resolve them.
- Treating all evidence as client-safe.
- Overusing raw quotes where synthesis would be more constructive.
- Presenting scenarios without conditions for success.
- Describing conflict without a mechanism.
- Turning values into slogans instead of rules or behaviors.
- Making the advisor sound replaced by the document instead of supported by it.
