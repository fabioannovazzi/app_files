## Design Context

### Scope
This design context is the default for shared Mparanza public and product
surfaces, especially the homepage, authentication, public plugin pages, and the
common page shell.

Specialized plugin review workbenches, the research toolkit, and Clara advisor
outputs have more specific overrides below because they serve different users
and workflow shapes.

### Users
The primary homepage visitor is a professional who is curious about applying
Codex to serious work. They may understand AI without yet knowing what a Codex
plugin is. The page must help them understand why the Codex harness changes what
AI can do, how Mparanza plugins make that harness useful for professional work,
and whether Vera or Clara fits their profession.

Inside product surfaces, users arrive with a clear task and use a focused
AI-assisted workflow in a serious work context. They are not looking for
entertainment, novelty, or broad exploration. The interface should help them
complete the task quickly and make the workflow feel reliable, composed, and
easy to trust.

### Product And Homepage Purpose
Mparanza is a point of view and a studio that builds plugins exclusively for
Codex. It turns specialist ways of working into plugins that make the Codex
harness useful for professional work. Mparanza is Vera and Clara. They are the
complete product pair, not the beginning of a series or unrelated items in a
catalogue.

The homepage should be one continuous scrolling page. It should establish the
Mparanza belief, define control specifically as the Codex harness, explain why
Mparanza builds only for Codex, and then introduce Vera and Clara on that same
page. A visitor should understand both products without having to open their
detail pages.

For each product, answer three questions concisely: who it is for, what work it
does, and what the plugin enables Codex to produce. Separate pages may provide
installation instructions and deeper detail.

The governing argument is:

> AI has power. Codex provides control: a harness that can work with files,
> tools, instructions, and project context. Mparanza builds plugins exclusively
> for Codex because a plugin gives that harness a specialist way of working and
> makes AI useful for a profession.

Better grounding, repeatability, inspection, and easier reduction of
hallucinations may result from that structure, but they are not part of the
homepage argument and must not appear as a supporting claim. Do not reinterpret
control as human supervision, professional responsibility, or a generic warning
not to trust AI; those ideas do not define or differentiate Mparanza.

### Brand Personality
Elegant, professional, restrained.

The desired emotional result is confidence, calm, and agency. Mparanza should
feel like a considered professional position, not a technology demonstration.

The product should feel calm, sharp, and edited. It should not sound or look
salesy, playful, or self-congratulatory. The interface should make advanced
workflows feel normal and usable without leaning on hype.

### Aesthetic Direction
Default to light mode. The visual direction should be quiet and refined, with
restrained neutrals, disciplined spacing, and strong typography.

The right reference point is closer to the calmer OpenAI product/editorial
surfaces than to a generic AI startup dashboard. Avoid visual BS: gimmicky
gradients, glossy cards, decorative shadows, noisy pills, overloaded status
chips, loud accent colors, and generic "AI" motifs.

The UI should feel elegant enough for professional work: minimal but not cold,
clean but not sterile, and polished without becoming ornamental.

The homepage should feel like a compact editorial argument rather than a SaaS
landing page, dashboard, or plugin marketplace. Use a continuous narrative,
generous whitespace, strong live typography, and thin rules. Use a clean white
canvas and do not tint the homepage background. Typography and structure must
carry the explanation without relying on decorative artwork.

### Design Principles
1. Lead with the belief, then make it concrete. Advanced AI becomes useful for
   professional work when a clear Codex workflow gives it purpose, evidence,
   and reviewable outputs.
2. Present Vera and Clara as the complete Mparanza pair and proof of the
   Mparanza idea. Keep both on the same scrolling homepage and give each enough
   context to be understood before a visitor follows a detail or installation
   link.
3. Explain plugins in professional language before technical language. Start
   with the work, the user, the specialist method, and the useful output rather
   than package mechanics.
4. Prefer concrete proof over broad claims. Use real workflow mechanics and a
   small number of representative outputs; do not claim adoption, customer
   outcomes, or testimonials without evidence.
5. Prefer elegance over emphasis. If something can be quieter without losing
   clarity, make it quieter.
6. Keep AI implicit as a visual theme. Show usefulness, structure, and control
   instead of relying on generic AI imagery or effects.
7. Make every word earn its place. Avoid hype, filler, and self-promotional
   copy.
8. Use a small number of clear visual signals. Hierarchy should come from
   typography, spacing, and alignment before color or decoration.
9. Design for professional trust. Interfaces should feel edited, stable, and
   intentional rather than trendy or experimental.
10. Avoid numbered workflow cards or prominent `1, 2, 3, 4` step badges on
   professional pages. Prefer semantic labels, grouped panels, or unnumbered
   lists unless exact legal/procedural sequencing is the substance of the
   content.
11. Public plugin pages should use user-facing product language, not internal
   package names. For the shared plugin ZIP, say that one ZIP installs all
   Mparanza plugins.

## Plugin Review Workbench Override

### Scope
This override applies to Codex plugin review widgets and local MCP HTML
surfaces, especially commercialista and professional review workflows such as
Check Entries, Audit Reconciliation, Journal Sampling, Journal-Bank
Reconciliation, Client Intake, Report Builder, Prompt Optimizer, Concordato
Plan Review, and Deep Research validation.

These are working review desks, not dashboards, landing pages, or demo pages.
They should open directly into the review task and help the user make and
persist decisions.

### Users
Commercialisti, accounting professionals, reviewers, and adjacent professional
users are checking evidence, exceptions, source support, document requests, and
final artifacts under time pressure.

They need control, traceability, and confidence. They are not browsing for
insight or evaluating a product pitch; they are deciding whether to accept,
reject, edit, mark unclear, request documents, skip, or apply review decisions.

### Brand Personality
Quiet, exact, professional.

The interface should feel like a clean professional review table: composed,
legible, precise, and calm. It should never feel like a generic AI dashboard or
a decorative analytics widget.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use a white background by default. Prefer a mostly white canvas with subtle
line-based separation, disciplined spacing, and compact typography.

Avoid tinted page backgrounds, green/slate dashboard themes, decorative
gradient surfaces, glossy cards, shadow stacks, bokeh/orb decoration, noisy
status pills, and large colored blocks. Color should be sparse and functional:
mainly for state, risk, and the single primary action.

The closest reference is a restrained OpenAI-style editor/review surface:
quiet white workspace, strong type, low visual noise, and clear interaction
states.

### Layout Rules
1. Prefer line-based structure over card-heavy structure. Use cards only for
   repeated review rows, focused detail panes, and actual framed editors.
2. Do not nest cards inside cards. Flatten detail views with sections, rules,
   and spacing.
3. Keep the first screen work-focused: queue, selected item, evidence, decision
   controls, and final outputs. No marketing hero or explanatory feature text.
4. Use dense but organized layouts. Professional tools should support scanning,
   comparison, and repeated action.
5. Keep one primary action. Save/apply may be prominent, but secondary actions
   should be quiet.
6. Empty states may explain what is missing, but should stay short and practical.

### Typography Rules
1. Use a fixed app-UI type scale, not viewport-scaled type.
2. Body text should be at least `1rem`; compact labels may be smaller only when
   hierarchy and legibility remain clear.
3. Use weight and spacing before color for hierarchy. Avoid heavy bold text
   everywhere.
4. Headings inside workbench panels should be compact, not hero-sized.
5. Use tabular numerals for metrics and aligned numbers.
6. Keep copy short, active, and operational. Do not describe obvious UI
   features in visible text.

### Control And State Rules
1. Decision controls must support accept, reject, edit, mark unclear, request
   more documents, and skip when the workflow contract allows them.
2. The UI must make pending, reviewed, accepted, needs evidence, final-ready,
   blocked, and partial states visible without shouting.
3. Status pills should be small, low-saturation, and functional. Avoid pill
   overload.
4. Evidence views must be workflow-specific, not raw JSON dumps, unless no
   structured view is possible.
5. The user must be able to write back through the MCP path: save decisions,
   apply decisions, and persist durable review artifacts.

## Research Toolkit Override

### Scope
This override applies only to the research toolkit surfaces:

- `/research/prompt/page`
- `/research/validate/page`

and to closely related UI work under the `/research` toolkit.

### Users
Independent legal and tax professionals use these two research toolkit pages.
That includes independent legal advisors, independent lawyers, and adjacent
solo professionals who want a more structured way to ask a research question
and then pressure-test the answer they receive.

On `/research/prompt/page`, their job is to shape a research request clearly
enough to get a useful Deep Research output.

On `/research/validate/page`, their job is not exhaustive source auditing of
every link. It is to reduce risk by structuring the review around a limited set
of core claims and checking those claims one by one.

### Brand Personality
Precise, sober, controlled.

The research toolkit should feel like a careful review desk: calm under
pressure, methodical, and serious enough for legal work without feeling
corporate or heavy.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use a forensic editorial tone rather than a generic AI-product dashboard. The
research toolkit should feel like a working professional surface for drafting
and review, with clear status signals, restrained color, strong typographic
hierarchy, and visible structure.

Avoid anything playful, glossy, or obviously "AI assistant" themed.

### Design Principles
1. Scope assumptions carefully: audience, tone, and workflow assumptions here
   apply to the research toolkit only, not to unrelated modules in the repo.
2. Make the method visible: show how the toolkit structures the research task,
   whether in prompt creation or in claim-first validation.
3. Optimize for trust, not spectacle: emphasize clear statuses, explanations,
   and review traceability over decorative UI.
4. Keep the user oriented: always make it obvious what the current task is,
   what is in focus, and what still needs review.
5. Preserve control: the user remains the final professional reviewer, and the
   interface should reinforce that this is decision support rather than
   automated truth.

## Clara / Advisor Output Override

### Scope
This override applies to Clara-generated working artifacts for senior advisors:
HTML partner briefs, HTML decks, decision-pack storyboards, exhibit-style
tables, and future deck outputs used with a boutique advisory/governance firm.

It does not make case-specific facts part of the product. Client names,
advisor names, family names, and engagement details belong only in local case
workspaces.

Clara's advisor artifacts do not need to use Mparanza visual language. They
should stand on their own as professional advisory outputs.

### Users
Senior consultants and partners use these outputs while preparing advisory,
growth, governance, and succession work for entrepreneurs, ADs, family
businesses, and management teams.

They need material that feels like serious partner leverage: clear issue
framing, concise exhibits, practical workplans, and decision-ready synthesis.
They are usually time-constrained and already bring professional judgement, so
the output should help them think faster rather than teach them generic
consulting theory.

### Brand Personality
Professional, quiet, partner-grade.

The tone should feel composed, selective, and useful. It should convey
confidence through structure and restraint, not through decoration, slogans, or
consulting theater.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use light-mode, editorial, boardroom-ready layouts: white canvas, restrained
warm/green neutrals, black or near-black headings, thin rules, compact tables,
and disciplined spacing.

HTML decks and partner briefs should feel like a careful boutique advisory
artifact: refined but not luxury, structured but not corporate boilerplate,
quiet but not empty. Avoid glossy gradients, large decorative cards, loud icon
sets, generic AI motifs, colorful dashboards, and over-designed presentation
effects.

Until a firm template is supplied, default to a calm boutique-advisory
direction: high-trust typography, sparse accent color, clear exhibit titles,
and practical tables that can be discussed directly with a partner. Do not
force Mparanza branding, plugin page styling, or product UI conventions into
Clara's partner-facing work.

### Design Principles
1. Lead with the decision. Every section, page, or slide should make clear what
   issue it helps the partner resolve.
2. Prefer exhibits over prose. Use issue trees, role maps, option matrices,
   workplans, risk registers, evidence gaps, and project-control tables.
3. Keep it quiet. Use typography, spacing, and thin rules before color,
   shadows, icons, or decorative panels.
4. Separate source from judgement. Make facts, hypotheses, partner judgement,
   open questions, and client-ready conclusions visibly distinct.
5. Respect the partner's role. The artifact should support a senior advisor's
   judgement and voice, not sound like an autonomous AI recommendation.
6. Design for reuse in decks. Tables and exhibits should have concise titles,
   stable columns, clear units/statuses, and enough whitespace to be lifted
   into a later PowerPoint or HTML deck without redesign.

### Internal Working Document Rules
1. Internal partner documents should be the review object, not a description of
   what Clara will later produce. Replace meta-instructions with provisional
   judgement, assumptions, review questions, and decision asks.
2. State the internal/private nature once, preferably in the document status or
   eyebrow. Do not repeat "not for the client" and "for the partner" in the same
   opening block.
3. The first screen should orient the partner to action: what is emerging, what
   needs correction, and what decision or next draft the document supports.
4. Use a 10-12 page structure only when each page has a distinct decision job:
   direction, assumptions, scenario matrix, operating transfer, governance,
   failure modes, evidence map, and partner decisions.
5. Include an assumptions register for important internal drafts. Each
   assumption should say why it matters, current confidence, and the test or
   partner correction required.
6. Avoid generic pro/con framing. Scenario pages should specify decision
   rights, conflict rules, evidence, operating implications, success gates, and
   failure triggers.
7. Use review language, not production language. Prefer "the partner should
   validate/correct/decide" over "Clara should produce" inside the artifact.
8. End with a concrete partner decision list. A useful internal document should
   make it easy for the partner to say yes, no, deeper here, wrong assumption,
   or ready for the next draft.
